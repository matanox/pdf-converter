Under proﬁt-based transfer pricing methods, the selection of comparable companies is essential
if detection of transfer price manipulation is to be reliable. Comparative advantage as
embedded in internalisation theory argues that foreign-controlled companies (FCCs) should,
in the long run, display greater proﬁtability than domestic-controlled companies. In high-tax
host countries, transfer pricing manipulation theory predicts an opposite effect on
proﬁtability. Applying a reﬁned set of tests to a large sample of ﬁrms operating in a high-tax
country such as Italy offers strong support for the internalisation prediction. Furthermore,
the analysis of the interquartile range of our measure of proﬁtability indicates that only a
low percentage of FCCs would be subject to ﬁscal enquires, as implied by the Organisation
for Economic Co-operation and Development guidelines, under the suspicious of transfer
pricing manipulation. These results suggest that current comparability tests are likely to fail
the identiﬁcation of transfer pricing practices in countries where the comparative advantage
of FCCs is particularly pronounced and question the reliability of these tests.
Keywords: transfer pricing; reliability; comparables; proﬁt-based methods

Transfer pricing manipulation theory posits that multinational enterprises have incentives to
over- or under-invoice their intra-group transfers in the presence of corporate income tax differ-
entials between host and home country (Lin et al. 1993, Eden 1998). Speciﬁcally, afﬁliates oper-
ating in a high-tax country are expected to show lower proﬁtability than domestic companies due
to either artiﬁcially deﬂated taxable revenues or inﬂated deductible expenses relating to intra-
group trade.
Several methods are proposed to verify transfer pricing compliance (see the OECD (2010a,
2010b) and UN (2011) model conventions). The search for a set of comparable companies
under these methods becomes essential for the detection of manipulations in intra-group trans-
actions in multinational corporations and this is particularly true when the comparable proﬁts
or the transactional net margin methods are employed. Here, the focus to verify transfer
pricing compliance moves from individual transactions to the proﬁts of comparable companies.
The careful selection of comparable companies is vital to demonstrate that any differences in prof-
itability between a tested party and unrelated parties are realistically attributable to transfer prices
not in line with the arm’s length proﬁt principle. This procedure poses particular challenges since
price is only one component of proﬁt. Independent taxpayers undertaking similar activities and
functions may exhibit different cost structures and systems, operate in different geographic
locations or markets, the quality of management may differ as well as capital costs, corporate
objectives, business efﬁciency and experience.
The purpose of this study is to test whether the use of comparable proﬁt-based methods can
reliably detect international transfer pricing manipulation in an institutional setting where a high
corporate tax rate is linked with a business environment where afﬁliates of multinational enter-
prises are expected to display strong comparative advantages relative to domestic companies.
Concerns on the implications of the transfer pricing manipulation theory, and the assumption
that where assets are located should reﬂect the expected return (Wheeler 1988, 1990), motivated
numerous empirical studies which compare foreign-controlled companies (FCCs) and domestic-
controlled companies (DCCs) proﬁtability to demonstrate that opportunistic income-shifting
activities were in place (Wheeler 1988, Kim and Lyn 1990, Grubert and Mutti 1991, Harris
et al. 1991, Grubert et al. 1993, Crain and Stitts 1994, Collins et al. 1997, Grubert 1997,
Collins et al. 1998). Early contributions based on the USA correlate differences in proﬁtability
with differentials in corporate tax rates and import tariffs to infer that aggressive transfer
pricing is the cause of DCCs outperforming FCCs. Similar arguments characterise a few empirical
studies that collect evidence on Europe by focusing on the UK, Norway and Germany (Oyelere
and Emmanuel 1998, Langli and Saudagaran 2004, Weichenrieder 2009).

The interpretation of these ﬁnding s as an indication of the presence of international transfer
pricing anomalies in multinational afﬁliates rests on internalisation theory, a long-established
explanation of the existence and growth of multinational enterprises (Dunning 1958, Caves
1996). In order to engage in successful foreign direct investments, multinational enterprises
should have superior technological, managerial or other intangible advantages over domestic
ﬁrms. In the long term, their subsidiaries beneﬁt from these comparative advantages after familiar-
isation with the host countries’ culture, business practices and customer preferences in the start-up
period. Accordingly, lower levels of proﬁtability in FCCs than in DCCs contradict the importance
of comparative advantage underpinning an international strategy and provides evidence to
support the anomaly as in the international transfer pricing studies.
The possible interaction between the comparative advantages and international transfer
pricing manipulation incentives may explain why the literature focusing on comparative advan-
tage theory offers mixed results on the expected superiority of FCCs (Globerman et al. 1994,
Doms and Jensen 1998, Girma et al. 2001, Harris 2002, Harris and Robinson 2003, Benfratello
and Sembenelli 2006).
In this study we suggest that the interaction between the incentives to undertake international
transfer pricing manipulations and the comparative advantage enjoyed by the FCCs might produce
a less clear-cut picture than that postulated by the transfer pricing manipulation theory and may,
then, undermine the ability of comparability methods to detect transfer pricing manipulations.
We undertake a proﬁt-comparability analysis to detect the presence of international transfer
pricing manipulations at the ﬁrm level. Essentially, the purpose is to empirically investigate the
ﬁscal implications of using independent domestic companies as comparables to judge the congru-
ence of FCCs proﬁtability in one European jurisdiction.
To this end, we focus on a sample of Italian companies. The choice of Italy is motivated by the
peculiarities of the Italian institutional framework which is markedly different from those charac-
terising other countries analysed in previous international transfer pricing studies. In particular,
Italy shows the two characteristics that may generate conﬂicting predictions between the transfer
pricing manipulation theory and the internalisation theory: (i) a high level of corporate taxation;
(ii) a low level of foreign direct investment attractiveness. In spite of various ﬁscal reforms, Italy
remains characterised by high statutory corporate tax rates and high effective corporate tax rates
as compared to the majority of other countries (Nicode

2. Proﬁt-based methods and comparability in international transfer pricing
Proﬁt-based methods, such as the comparable proﬁts and the transactional net margin methods,
use average industry rates of return on proﬁt to value the taxpayers’ proﬁts adherence to the
arm’s length principle (Eden 1998, p. 420). Both methods apply the “arm’s length” principle
through the examination of proﬁts – instead of focusing on prices – that arise from particular
transactions or activities (Culbertson 1995). The procedure involves testing one of the transacting
enterprises’ proﬁt-level indicators (such as ROA or operating proﬁt to costs or sales) with those
earned by unrelated enterprises (OECD 2010a, 2010b, par. 2.58; Income Tax Regulation, Section
1.482– 5). When the tested enterprise’s ﬁnancial results fall outside the comparable arm’s length
range, a ﬁscal enquiry leading to a possible audit is triggered.
2
The search for comparable companies plays the heroic role of guaranteeing that they enjoy
market positions, levels of efﬁciency, advanced technology and so on that are similar to those of
the tested party in order to realistically infer that eventual differences in proﬁts relate to abuse in
transfer pricing (Emmanuel and Mehafdi 1994). In making the comparison between intra-group
and unrelated party transactions not only the internalisation concept but also value chain, market
versus hierarchy, strategic management and multinational enterprises as strategi c oligopolists con-
siderations may be taken into account (Eden 1998). The different acceptable methods can yield
different proﬁts for subsidiaries or afﬁliates “since no method is suitable in every possible situation”
(OECD 1994, par. 84). The previous recommendation of the OECD favoured the exercise of good
judgement, selecting a method and transfer price that best ﬁts the facts and circumstances of the
case. However, since the current advice is to choose the most reliable transfer pricing method
(OECD 2010a, 2010b), the transactional net margin method is no longer a method of last resort.
Reliability therefore occupies a fulcrum position in policing transfer pricing manipulation, simul-
taneously enabling international trade and development.

of the Italian Income Tax Code), whose deﬁnition substantially coincides with the OECD arm’s
length principle. The International Standard Ruling Report issued by the Italian ﬁscal authority

3. Hypotheses development
The transfer price manipulation theory (Eden 1998), regardless of the level of taxation or the
degree of attractiveness for foreign direct investment in the host country, requires transfer
prices in line with the arm’s length standard. When FCCs equalise the performance of indepen-
dent comparable companies, this theory recognises the transfer pricing practices of these compa-
nies as congruent with the arm’s length principle.
By contrast, the presence of signiﬁcant differences in ﬁnancial performance between FCCs
and DCCs is normally interpreted as offering an indirect evidence of potential transfer pricing
abuse: it is a manifestation of income-shifting strategies for tax-minimisation purposes (Emma -
nuel and Oyelere 2002, p. 4). This is because, according to the transfer price manipulation
theory, a multinational company is expected to over-state intra-group exports and under-state
intra-ﬁrm imports in order to minimise its global tax liability when the host country presents a
level of taxation higher than the parent company (Lin et al. 1993). Therefore, foreign subsidiaries
will report, ceteris paribus, a lower proﬁt than domestic companies due to net effect of either max-
imising tax deductible expenses or minimising tax revenues through the alteration of transfer
prices.
Obviously, the incentives for an FCC to engage in income-shifting strategies are expected to
be especially pronounced in an institutional context where corporate taxation is strongly penalis-
ing in the host country as is the case of Italy. In sum, under the transfer price manipulation theory
we formulate the following hypothesis:
H
1
: Foreign controlled companies report signiﬁcantly lower proﬁtability than domestic independent
companies operating in Italy as they undertake international transfer pricing manipulations.
Empirical evidence which supports this hypothesis would imply that FCCs are artiﬁcially manip-
ulating transfer prices to shift proﬁts outside Italy as postulated by transfer pricing manipulation
theory. As a consequence, proﬁt-based comparability methods will be able to detect international
transfer pricing manipulation to the extent they materialise in terms of differences in proﬁtability
between FCCs and DCCs.
However, we suggest that in a higher-taxed host country, as is the case of Italy, foreign afﬁli-
ates may still present higher proﬁtability levels than domestic companies due to the presence of
substantial comparative advantages (Buckley and Casson 2009). Thus, proﬁt-based comparability
methods will not provide a signal to ﬁscal authorities on the presence of abuse in international
transfer pricing practices. We postulate the following alternative hypothesis:
H
2
: Foreign controlled companies report signiﬁcantly higher proﬁtability than domestic independent
companies operating in Italy due to their comparative advantages.
Empirical evidence that supports this second hypothesis will open to question the reliability of the
comparison with no negative ﬁscal implications for these companies. A higher proﬁt, though not
in line with comparable companies, will be accepted by the local ﬁscal authorities as it generates
higher tax revenues for the host country. In sum, under proﬁt-based methods these companies
have scope for manipulating international transfer pricing without facing ﬁscal consequences

foreign afﬁliates without arousing the attention of the ﬁscal authority.
4. Sample, variable construction and statistical tests
4.1 The sampling process
The sample of DCCs and FCCs was drawn from the database AIDA, managed by Bureau van
Dijk, which contains detailed ﬁnancial accounting and ownership data on a large number of com-
panies located in Italy. In order to ensure that the sample properly represents the Italian productive
sector, and since AIDA has substantially increased the amount of available information only from
the late 1990s, the sampling process has focused on the period spanning 1998– 2010. The
substantial number of Italian companies captured in th e database and the introduction of
proﬁt-based transfer pricing methods prompted the selection of this time period. In effect, the
comparable proﬁts method was ﬁrst introduced in the USA in 1992, and a few years later the
OECD ofﬁcially introduced the transactional net margin method as did the UN, with a similar
set of rules to those of the comparable proﬁts method (Culbertson 1995).
The ﬁnal sample is the result of the application of several selection criteria. First, due to their
speciﬁcities in terms of business policies and strategies, the sample does not include ﬁnancial,
real estate ﬁrms and ﬁ r ms with act i vities related t o the public sector. Second, coopera tive ﬁrms as
non-proﬁt-maximising enti ties are also excluded from th e samp le that, therefore, consists of only
limited liability corporations. Third, we require that all companies close their ﬁscal year at the
end of the calendar year . Fourth, with the purpose of both eliminating concerns over the reliability
of the accounting items provided at the ﬁrm level and excluding unimportant ﬁrms in ﬁnancial terms,
we maintain in the sample only ﬁrms with a positive book-equity value (as in Klassen et al. 1993)
and with a minimum value of total sales equal to E1,000,000. Fi fth, we maintain in the sample only
ﬁrms for which we can clearly identify the nationality of the owner from the database. Finally, we
remove f rom the s ample DCCs th at are not independent entities as they are c ontrolled by another
corporation that holds at least 50% of the total ownership. Applying these criteria allows our selec-
tion process to conform to the approach adopted by ﬁscal authorities in identifying comparables
when the purpose is to detect international transfer pricing manipulation practices.

The model is estimated using a median regression so as to replicate in a multivariate frame-

regressions belong to the family of quantile regressions that aim to estimate the quantiles (in
our case the median) of the conditional distribution of the response variable (ROA) as functions
of the observed covariates. Koenker and Hallock (2001) show that quantile regression is equiv-
alent to minimising a sum of asymmetrically weighted absolute residuals by means of a linear
programming method. While the estimation method mirrors our univariate approach, to ease
the comparison with previous studies, we also report the results using the more conventional
ordinary least squares (OLS) estimator.

of the univariate analysis. First, in our sample FCCs outperform DCCs in terms of ROA. While
this result is in contrast with the evidence found by several studies conducted in other institutional
settings (USA, Germany, UK and Norway) to detect the presence of income-shifting policies in
FCCs, it offers support for the internalisation theory and the related comparative advantage
hypothesis. In sum, this result suggests that eith er tax minimisation in Italy is not the aim of
FCCs or, more likely, if this is the case, the expected negative inﬂuence on proﬁtability is

policies and size are all key drivers of performances of companies based in Italy. These results
imply that the comparison of the degree of proﬁtability between FCCs and DCCs by ﬁscal auth-
orities needs to consider this full set of variables in the search for reliable comparables between
the two groups of ﬁrms.

5.4 The interquartile range of the ROA distribution
Our univariate and multivariate tests provide robust evidence of the presence of a signiﬁcant and
positive difference in median performances between FCCs and DCCs in Italy and this is in line
with the postulates of the internalisation theory.
However, the comparability of the performances of a te sted company with those of unrelated
independent companies to detect transfer pricing manipulations, as generally conducted by ﬁscal
authorities in accordance with the transfer pricing guidelines established by the OECD (2010a,
2010b), may go beyond the analysis of the median values of a measure of performance. For
instance, in the presence of a great number of observations it is suggested that the interquartile
range or other percentiles will help in the search of comparables to justify the arm’s length range.
In the context of the current analysis, the possibility to use additional analytical tools by ﬁscal
authorities is particularly relevant as it might indicate that, in spite of the presence of a relevant
comparative advantage in the median foreign ﬁrm, there is scope to activate transfer pricing
enquires via the adoption of more reﬁned approaches. These enquires can then collect more infor-
mation to uncover manipulations in transfer prices that otherwise would be ignored under the
comparison of median performances.

interquartile range as an assessment method does not seem effective even for FCCs where the
comparative advantages do not fully materialise and the reported proﬁtability levels are unlikely
to induce any ﬁscal enquiry.
6. Conclusions
Our ﬁndings contradict the traditional evidence in the international transfer pricing empirical lit-
erature (see for example Collins et al. 1997, Grubert 1997, Collins et al. 1998, Emmanuel and
Oyelere 2002, Langli and Saudagaran 2004) by showing that FCCs outperform their Italian inde-
pendent counterparts at a sta tistically signiﬁcant level. This result holds when alternative proﬁt-
level indicators and more precise comparators are incorpo rated within the analysis. Furthermore,
the application of the interquartile range to detect acceptable arm’s length proﬁts highlights that a
high proportion of FCCs either reports performance within the interquartile range of the ROA dis-
tribution or above. Unexpectedly, in a country with an extremely high taxation, the comparability
method, even in its more reﬁned approach, suggests that transfer pricing manipulation is not a
major issue.
A possible explanation for our results can be related to the speciﬁcities of Italy in terms of
level of taxation, foreign direct investment attractiveness and legal enforcement: the performance
differential appears a plausible consequence of strong comparative advantages that FCCs operat-
ing in Italy may enjoy. Thus, the expectation of a similar proﬁtability between a given tested FCC
and a set of comparable independent companies as a proof that transfer prices are set at arm’s
length appears potentially fallacious.
The presence of these comparative advantages might depend on a lower quality of DCCs man-
agement and a consequent lower business efﬁciency. Furthermore, the magnitude of this advan-
tage in terms of performance differential might be inﬂated by a greater aggressiveness in tax
avoidance by DCCs. In this respect, we speculate that especially small and medium-sized
Italian ﬁrms, often individually or family owned, might be more ﬂexible and effective in exploit-
ing a low level of tax enforcement and thus hiding their proﬁtability given the absence of strong
outside shareholders (Desai et al. 2007).
On the basis of our results, we argue that the capability of tax authorities to detect and police
transfer price manipulation has to rely more heavily on judgement than apparently “hard” ﬁnan-
cial data and this provides an explanation for the investigation of individual intra-group

transactions and activities. The exercise of judgement by ﬁscal agents becomes of paramount
importance but, as Ernst & Young surveys (2011) have shown, the preferred methods for intan-
gibles and intellectual property activities are the comparable proﬁts and transactional net margin
methods. These are precisely the intra-group activities where comparative advantages come to the
fore and where internalisation legitimises proﬁtability differentials. They are also activities where
even inexact comparables may not exist or can be contested.
The lack of focus on individual transactions or individual FCCs represents a limitation of this
study. In addition, the analysis ignores the total tax regime operating in Italy and does not attempt
to recognise the importance of intra-group transactions involving DCCs with differing degrees of
international involvement. Nevertheless, we show that a careful analysis of comparables using the
reﬁned indirect method and the OEC D-recommended method gives rise to ambiguous interpret-
ations. The reliability of the tests of comparability therefore seems a reasonable starting point for
debate on whether the arm’s length principle should remain the cornerstone of ﬁscal regulation.
The recent proposal of the European Commission to introduce the Common Consolidated
Corporate Tax Base to tax multinational enterprises operating in Europe may, arguably, bypass
all potential disputes over internal transfer prices. An alternative view is that the comparables
problem is then replaced by a formula apportionment problem that is equally intractable.
Nonetheless, in their indifference towards detailed disclosure of international transfer pricing
policies, accounting standard setters appear surprisingly convergent across countries.

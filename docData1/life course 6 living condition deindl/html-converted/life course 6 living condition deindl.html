Introduction
A
lot
has
been
written
about
the
association
between
socioeconomic
status
–
income,
wealth,
education
–
and
wellbeing.
The
results
from
these
studies
indicate
that
a
favourable
position
in
society
is
related
to
higher
levels
of
wellbeing.
People
with
a
higher
income
are
more
satisﬁed
with
their
lives
(e.g.,
Diener
&
Oishi,
2000;
George,
2010),
live
longer
(e.g.,
Phelan,
Link,
Diez-Roux,
Kawachi,
&
Levin,
2004),
and
have
a
better
health
(e.g.,
Kim,
Sargent-Cox,
French,
Kendig,
&
Anstey,
2011)
than
people
with
a
lower
income.
Apart
from
the
positive
association
between
socioeco-
nomic
status
(SES)
and
wellbeing
on
the
micro
level,
the
wealth
of
a
country
among
other
country
characteristics
is
also
important
(Bo
¨
hnke,
2008;
Diener
&
Oishi,
2000;
Heukamp
&
Arin
˜
o,
2011;
Wilkinson
&
Pickett,
2010;
Zheng,
2012).
In
addition
to
the
net
effect
of
country
conditions,
the
connection
between
income
and
life
satisfaction
is
less
pronounced
in
wealthier
countries,
although
there
are
still
differences
between
the
rich
and
the
poor
in
these
countries
(Diener,
Suh,
Lucas,
&
Smith,
1999).
There
are
also
studies
dealing
with
the
relationship
between
life
events
and
satisfaction
such
as
marriage
dissolution,
becoming
unemployed
or
getting
married
(Headey
&
Wearing,
1989;
Lucas,
Clark,
Georgellis,
&
Diener,
2003).
Apart
from
life
course
events
a
long
term
inﬂuence
of
living
conditions
in
early
life
on
successful
ageing
(Brandt,
Deindl,
&
Hank,
2012),
on
health
(Lawlor
et
al.,
2005),
depression
(Wickrama,
Noh,
&
Elder,
2009),
and
mortality
(Palloni,
Milesi,
White,
&
Turner,
2009)
can
be
found.
Astonishingly,
the
impact
of
early
life
socio-
economic
status
on
life
satisfaction
has
–
to
the
best
of
our
knowledge
–
not
been
studied
explicitly
yet,
and
it
is
an
open
question
if
a
comparable
impact
of
early
adversity
can
be
found.
Contrary
to
other
measures
of
wellbeing
(e.g.,
health,
depression
or
successful
ageing),
life
satisfaction
is
seen
as
a
relatively
stable
concept
that
is
not
dramatically
affected
by
positive
or
negative
life
events
(e.g.,
Brickman,
Coates,
&
Janoff-Bulman,
1978;
George,
2010),
even
if
the
adaptation
of
life
satisfaction
is
not
‘‘perfect’’
after
unfavourable
life
events
(Diener,
Lucas,
&
Scollon,
2006).
Looking
at
the
connection
between
childhood
conditions
and
life
satis-
faction
can
therefore
inform
us
about
the
formation
of
the
individual
satisfaction
level.
For
that
reason,
we
examine
the
inﬂuence
of
early
life
socio-economic
status
on
life
satisfaction
in
old
age.

life
living
conditions
are
measured
on
two
levels.

2.
The
inﬂuence
of
early
life
living
conditions
on
life
satisfaction
in
old
age
There
are
two
main
models
on
how
early
life
can
inﬂuence
later
life
outcomes:
the
pathway
and
the
latency
model
(see
e.g.,
Haas,
2008;
Zhang,
Gu,
&
Hayward,
2008).
The
latency
model
supposes
a
direct
link
between
early
life
on
later
life
outcomes,
where
exposure
to
unfavourable
health
conditions
has
a
long
lasting
impact
on
health
in
old
age
(Hertzman
&
Power,
2004).
The
pathway
model
differentiates
between
direct
and
indirect
inﬂuences
and
assumes
that
early
adversity
impacts
wellbeing
mainly
through
indirect
pathways.
For
example,
a
low
SES
during
childhood
results
in
a
low
SES
during
adult
life
that
has
a
direct
inﬂuence
on
wellbeing
in
old
age
(e.g.,
Case,
Fertig,
&
Paxson,
2005).
The
view
that
disadvantages
in
early
life
have
a
long
lasting
impact
on
wellbeing
in
old
age
can
be
combined
with
the
theory
of
cumulative
inequality,
that
is
also
called
cumulative
disadvantage
theory
(Schafer,
Ferraro,
&
Mustillo,
2011,
p.
1056).
According
to
cumulative
disadvantage
theory,
‘‘statuses
and
events
from
early
points
in
the
life
course
pave
the
road
to
an
individual’s
future,
creating
a
divergence
between
individuals
that
expands
over
the
course
of
time’’
(Schafer
et
al.,
2011,
p.
1056).
This
process
of
divergence
between
individuals
can
be
described
by
two
different
processes
(DiPrete
&
Eirich,
2006).
The
ﬁrst
approach
is
based
on
a
direct
link,
where
inequality
is
transmitted
directly
from
earlier
exposur e
to
later
life
inequality,
for
example
when
different
portfolios
produce
different
outcomes
if
the
int erest
rate
depends
upon
the
initial
amount
put
into
the
account,
and
thus
create
greater
wealth
inequality
ove r
time
(DiPrete
&
Eirich,
2006,
p.
273f.).
The
second
approach
follows
the
status
attainment
model
developed
by
Blau
and
Duncan
(1967)
and
distinguishes
between
direct
and
indirect
effects
of
initial
inequality.
For
example
‘‘growing
up
in
a
poor
versus
a
rich
family’’
or
single
versus
two-parent
families
provoke
an
accu-
mulation
of
inequality
ove r
time
(see
DiP rete
&
Eirich,
2006,
p.
273).
The
direct
approach
of
cumulative
inequality
is
similar
to
the
latency
model
where
an
initial
disadvantage
results
in
health
problems
decades
later
without
any
further
exposure
to
unfavourable
circumstances
(Hertzman
&
Power,
2004).
The
model
of
status
reproduction
with
its
emphasis
on
direct
and
indirect
pathways
is
closely
connected
to
the
pathway
model.
It
is
thus
obvious
that
there
are
not
necessarily
two
or
three
models
to
explain
the
connection
between
early
life
living
conditions
and
later
life
outcomes
(see
also
Hertzman
&
Power,
2004),
but
they
can
be
subsumed
under
the
framework
of
cumulative
inequality.
The
differences
between
the
direct
and
the
indirect
approach
lie
in
the
different
accentuation
of
the
underlying
dynamics.
The
main
focus
of
the
indirect
approach
of
cumulative
inequality
is
on
the
process
of
the
accumula-
tion
of
advantage
and
disadvantage
and
how
inequality
is
generated
over
the
life
course.
The
direct
approach
combines
the
relationship
between
exposure
to
disadvan-
tages
in
early
and
in
later
life
without
any
further
negative
or
positive
exposure
to
disadvantages
(DiPrete
&
Eirich,
2006;
Hertzman
&
Power,
2004;
Schafer
et
al.,
2011).
The
indirect
inﬂuence
of
early
living
conditions
on
wellbeing
in
later
life
formulated
in
the
pathway
model
can
be
modelled
as
follows:
While
the
social
status
of
parents
is
important
for
the
living
circumstances
in
early
life,
it
also
shapes
the
living
circumstance
in
later
life,
if
the
realized
status
depends
on
social
origin.
Since
life
satisfaction
is
inﬂuenced
by
socioeconomic
status
(e.g.,
Diener
&
Oishi,
2000),
it
also
depends
on
the
social
status
in
early
life.
Furthermore
a
process
of
accumulation
of
inequality
might
take
place
over
the
whole
life
course
when
different
interrelated
mechanisms
support
the
accumulation
of
advantages
or
disadvantages
(Ferraro
&
Shippee,
2009).
Cumulative
inequality
is
not
directly
connected
to
wellbeing
and
therefore
life
satisfaction,
it
is
merely
an
explanation
how
childhood
living
conditions
can
affect
living
conditions
in
later
life
and
therefore
inﬂuence
life
satisfaction
in
old
age.
Since
the
application
of
the
pathway
model
to
life
satisfaction
is
not
as
straightforward
as
concerning
other
indicators
of
wellbeing,
an
indirect
approach
is
necessary.
Childhood
living
conditions
have
an
indirect
inﬂuence
where
early
life
living
conditions
inﬂuence
living
conditions
later
in
life
and
therefore
also
have
an
impact
on
life
satisfaction.
Schafer
and
colleagues
suggest
that
early
disadvantages
inﬂuence
the
‘‘biographical
structuration’’
(Schafer
et
al.,
2011,
p.
1081)
and
have
a
negative
inﬂuence
on
the
perception
of
life.
This
view
is
in
line
with
the
reasoning
that
an
evaluation
of
previous
and
contemporary
social
positions
inﬂuences
future
action
and
therefore
the
possibilities
of
what
can
be
achieved
(Ferraro
&
Shippee,
2009).
Unfavourable
living
conditions
in
early
life
might
therefore
also
have
a
direct
negative
impact
on
wellbeing
in
later
life
since
the
evaluation
of
one’s
position
is
likely
to
be
negative.
If
there
is
a
direct
connection
between
early
life
and
wellbeing
and
additionally
an
indirect
pathway
via
living
conditions
in
later
life
in
accordance
with
cumula-
tive
inequality,
both
should
reinforce
each
other.
There
are
a
lot
of
additional
factors
that
have
been
shown
to
inﬂuence
life
satisfaction
or
subjective
wellbeing
such
as
socioeconomic
status,
health,
social
integration,
social
relationships,
social
support
and
psychological
resources
(George,
2010).
Diener
et
al.
(1999)
described
the
inﬂuence
of
income,
religion,
marriage,
age,
gender,
job,
and
education,
among
others
on
life
satisfaction.
It
can
be
assumed
that
these
factors
are
of
different
importance
in
different
periods
in
life.
While
emotional
closeness
to
parents
might
be
important
in
childhood
and
adolescence
(Schwarz
et
al.,
2012),
it
is
less
important
in
later
life
when

3.
Living
conditions
in
a
country
and
life
satisfaction
In
addition
to
micro
level
inﬂuences
of
living
conditions
in
early
life,
country
characteristics
also
have
an
impact
on
wellbeing.
It
is
well
known
that
people
in
poor
countries
are
less
healthy,
have
a
lower
life
expectancy
and
are
less
satisﬁed
with
their
lives
(Wilkinson
&
Pickett,
2007).
Different
living
standards
or
unequal
access
to
welfare
services
directly
affects
an
individuals’
wellbeing
in
a
country
(Ladin,
Daniels,
&
Kawachi,
2010).
In
addition,
personal
wellbeing
is
driven
by
the
subjective
comparison
with
others
(Diener
&
Oishi,
2000),
and
thus
relative
to
the
personal
surroundings
(Wilkinson
&
Pickett,
2007).
Being
wealthy
or
having
a
high
education
do
not
enhance
wellbeing
exclusively
as
such;
the
social
status
of
other
is
also
crucial:
being
rich
in
a
rich
country
is
thus
different
from
being
rich
in
a
poor
country,
and
therefore
country
level
factors
have
to
be
taken
into
account
(Bo
¨
hnke,
2008).
Furthermore,
living
conditions
in
a
country
during
childhood
may
impact
the
evaluation
of
one’s
own
childhood.
If
a
population
was
poor
during
childhood,
a
low
income
of
the
parents
matters
less
than
in
a
rich
country.
The
inﬂuence
of
a
poor
housing
standard
(no
access
to
warm
water,
etc.)
matters
less
if
the
neighbours
were
facing
the
same
problems.
Country
characteristics
also
shape
the
possibility
to
succeed
in
later
life.
There
are
countries
with
a
high
degree
of
stratiﬁcation,
where
social
mobility
is
rare,
and
other
societies
where
–
at
least
theoretically
–
everyone
has
the
same
access
to
education
and
therefore
early
disadvantages
should
be
ampliﬁed
less
over
the
life
course.
In
an
analysis
of
early
life
living
conditions
it
is
thus
crucial
to
take
country
characteristics
during
the
time
of
childhood
into
account.

Survey

Ageing
and

(SHARE).
The

of
SHARE
provides
information
about
living
conditions,

social
net-
works,
and
the

of

(Austria,

¨

The
SHARE
sample
is
representative
of
the
non-institutional-
ized
population
aged
50
and
over.
The
third
wave
of
SHARE,
the
so-called
SHARELIFE-survey
conducted
in

adds

about
the

of
the

(see

and
therefore
opens
the

to
assess
the
inﬂuence
of
living
conditions
in
childhood
on
life
satisfaction
in
old
age.
In
addition
to
data
from
SHARE,
measures
on
the
country
level
are
taken
from
Maddison
(2006)
who
collected
historical
information
on
the
Gross
Domestic
Product
(GDP)
per
country.
To
speciﬁcally
link
country
conditions
to
the
appropriate
childhood
conditions
cohorts
are
constructed
per
country
by
differentiating
ﬁve
groups
in
each
country
resulting
in
55
country-cohorts.
4.2.
Measures
4.2.1.
Dependent
variable
For
our
study
we
use
life
satisfaction
as
an
indicator
of
wellbeing,
as
this
measure
adds
to
the
studies
of
the
link
between
circumstances
in
early
life
and
wellbeing
in
later
life.
To
the
best
of
our
knowledge
there
is
no
study
yet
that
examines
the
inﬂuence
of
early
life
living
conditions
on
life
satisfaction.
Life
satisfaction
is
measured
using
the
standard
question:
‘‘On
a
scale
from
0
to
10
where
0
means
completely
dissatisﬁed
and
10
means
completely
satisﬁed,
how
satisﬁed
are
you
with
your
life?’’.
Despite
some
discussion
on
how
to
measure
subjective
wellbeing
(e.g.,
Schwarz
&
Strack,
1999),
the
eleven-point
scale
of
life
satisfaction
is
widely
used
and
has
been
shown
to
be
a
reliable
measure
of
subjective
wellbeing
(Diener
et
al.,
1999).
4.2.2.
Independent
variables
Childhood
living
conditions
are
classiﬁed
into
ﬁve
categories:
housing,
cultural
capital,
family
conditions,
health
status,
and
birth
cohort.
All
information
on
childhood
conditions
in
SHARELIFE
was
asked
for
the
age
of
ten.
Housing
conditions
are
operationalized
as
number
of
people
per
room
and
partly
captures
the
socio-
economic
background
of
the
family.
This
variable
was
created
by
the
author
by
dividing
the
number
of
people
in
the
household
by
the
number
of
rooms.
In
the
mean
there
are
1.77
people
per
room
in
our
analytic
sample
(see
Table
1).
Cultural
capital
is
measured
by
the
number
of
books
in
the
household
ranging
from
‘‘none
or
very
few
(0–10
books)’’
to
‘‘enough
to
ﬁll
two
or
more
bookcases
(more
than
200)’’.
Although
the
variable
number
of
books
is
merely
ordinal
in
scale
additional
analyses
show
that
it
can
be
treated
as
metric
variable
(see
also
Brandt
et
al.,
2012).
Family
conditions
are
operationalized
by
the
question
whether
respondents
lived
with
both
biological
parents,
which
were
the
case
for
the
majority
of
our
sample;
only
eight
per
cent
did
not
live
with
both
parents
when
they
were
ten.
Health
status
during
childhood
is
measured
using
self-rated
health
ranging
from
poor
(1)
to
excellent
(5).

Birth

distinguishes

ﬁve

1949

Two

The

It

A

On

It

1990

(see

We

and

(Czech-Republic

East-Germany
1949–1990,
Poland

1918).
For
Greece
information
about
GDP
in
the

1910–1920

only

Additional

and

1910–1920
for
Greece,

Apart

between

and

First,
we
included
the

status
measured
by
education
and

Education
distin-
guishes
between
low
(ISCED:
0–2),
medium
(ISCED:
3–4),
and
high
(ISCED:
5–6)
education.
Income
is
measured
as
the
log
of
the
PPP-adjusted

Partnership
was
measured

1

status
in
later
life
was
measured
by
self-reported
health
with
a
scale
ranging
from
1
(poor)
to
5
(excellent).
Additionally

was
used

control

The

for
later

All

Therefore

books
(cultural

income
to
people

room
(living
conditions),
health
in
later
life
to
health
in
childhood,
and
family
conditions
(living
with
both
biological
parents)
to

4.3.
Analytic
strategy
In
order
to
disentangle
the
inﬂuence
of
living
conditions
in
early
life
on
life
satisfaction
in
old
age,
stepwise
multilevel
models
using
the
Stata
module
GLLAMM
were
applied.
The
data
comprises
two
levels,
the
respondent
level
and
country-cohorts
on
the
second
level.
Since
possible
inﬂuences
of
country
characteristics
on
life
satisfaction
are
captured
by
country-cohorts,
the
addi-
tional
country
level
is
ignored
in
all
analyses.
The
necessity
of
multilevel
models
was
conﬁrmed
by
using
a
Likelihood
Ratio
Test
(using
the
Stata
command
xtmixed)
that
compares
the
multilevel
model
to
an
ordinary
linear
regression
model.
In
the
ﬁrst
step,
a
so-called
‘‘empty
model’’
was
calculated,
after
that,
childhood
and
contemporary
living
conditions
were
included.
In
a
next
step,
interaction
effects
were
introduced
to
disentangle
whether
contemporary
living
conditions
strengthen
or
weaken
the
inﬂuences
of
early
life
factors.
Finally,
a
model
with
all
main
and
interaction
effects
is
presented.

112
regard
to
life
satisfaction.
This
article
ﬁlls
this
gap
by
investigating
the
impact
of
early
life
conditions
(individual
and
country
characteristics)
on
life
satisfaction
in
old
age.
It
additionally
broadens
our
understanding
of
the
inﬂuence
of
country
characteristics
on
life
satisfaction
by
consider-
ing
the
impact
of
country
conditions
during
the
decade
of
birth.
The
results
show
that
living
conditions
in
early
life
have
a
long
lasting
inﬂuence
on
life
satisfaction
until
old
age.
This
is
in
line
with
research
on
health
and
successful
ageing:
both
indicators
of
wellbeing
are
positively
correlated
with
early
life
living
conditions
(e.g.,
Brandt
et
al.,
2012;
Lawlor
et
al.,
2005).
In
contrast
to
these
studies,
it
could
not
only
be
shown
that
there
is
a
correlation
between
early
and
late
life
characteristics,
we
furthermore
ascertained
that
there
is
an
interaction
between
childhood
conditions
and
income
in
later
life
that
is
important
for
life
satisfaction.
This
implies
that
there
is
also
an
indirect
effect
of
childhood
conditions
on
wellbeing
in
old
age,
where
being
in
a
favourable
position
during
the
childhood
years
is
also
associated
with
a
higher
income
later
on.
This
can
be
interpreted
as
sign
for
the
validity
of
the
pathway
model,
according
to
which
childhood
adversity
correlates
with
the
changes
to
achieve
a
speciﬁc
social
position
in
later
life.
Other
childhood
conditions,
for
example
health
status,
are
not
indirectly
correlated
with
life
satisfaction.
This
suggests
that
the
inﬂuence
of
socio-economic
conditions
is
more
important
in
the
long
run.
Childhood
conditions
on
the
individual
level
further-
more
interact
with
societal
conditions.
Favourable
condi-
tions
in
the
parental
household
mitigate
the
effects
of
adverse
country
conditions
and
the
other
way
around:
better
societal
conditions
can
also
compensate
for
unfavourable
individual
living
conditions
(see
also
Wilk-
inson
&
Pickett,
2007,
2010).
Apart
from
these
indirect
effects
on
life
satisfaction,
there
is
also
a
direct
link
between
life
satisfaction
and
living
conditions
in
early
life
with
signiﬁcant
correlations
even
after
controlling
for
contemporary
SES.
This
emphasizes
the
importance
of
considering
early
life
inﬂuences
in
research
on
life
satisfaction.
If
there
is
a
long
lasting
impact
of
early
adversity
on
wellbeing
in
old
age,
then
life
satisfaction
might
begin
to
form
quite
early
in
the
life
course.
The
inﬂuence
of
early
life
on
later
life
wellbeing
might
thus
be
in
the
‘‘biographical
structuration’’
as
suggested
by
Schafer
et
al.
(2011):
conditions
in
early
life
inﬂuence
how
we
see
our
opportunities
and
the
possibility
to
succeed
in
future.
However,
conditions
in
later
life,
like
income
or
health,
have
a
much
greater
impact
on
life
satisfaction.
That
means
early
life
circumstances
matter,
but
the
current
social
position
and
circumstances
are
more
important.
With
regard
to
the
theoretical
models
on
the
inﬂuences
of
early
life
on
later
life
conditions
this
indicates
that
the
pathway
model
is
appropriate
for
explaining
life
satisfaction
in
later
life.
Although
there
are
also
direct
effects
from
early
life
to
late
life,
the
coefﬁcients
become
much
smaller
once
contemporary
living
conditions
are
controlled
for.
Favour-
able
conditions
in
early
life
are
thus
‘‘inherited’’
from
parents
and
accumulated
over
the
life
course
resulting
in
better
living
conditions.
Unfortunately,
there
is
no
information
about
life
satisfaction
in
early-
or
midlife
available,
so
we
can
only
speculate
that
life
satisfaction
forms
early
in
life.
Further
research
is
needed
on
how
different
life
events,
like
unemployment,
divorce,
etc.,
affect
life
satisfaction
con-
trolling
for
living
conditions
in
early
life.
